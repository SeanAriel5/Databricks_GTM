Founding & Origins

Databricks was founded in 2013 by Ali Ghodsi, Matei Zaharia, and five fellow UC Berkeley researchers — the core team behind Apache Spark, the open-source distributed computing engine they built to address Hadoop's limitations. The founding insight was that data processing and analytics should be faster, simpler, and accessible across any cloud — a vision that would evolve from a Spark optimization layer into the world's leading Data Intelligence Platform.

The company pioneered the Data Lakehouse architecture — the convergence of data warehouses (structured, reliable, expensive, closed) and data lakes (cheap, flexible, ungoverned) onto open cloud object storage. Delta Lake, released open-source in 2019, provided the ACID transaction layer that made this convergence production-ready. By 2021, Databricks had established the Lakehouse as the dominant enterprise data architecture pattern, displacing the two-system (warehouse + lake) approach that had defined enterprise data infrastructure since the 1990s.

The Platform Evolution: From Spark Tool to Data Intelligence Platform

Databricks has gone through three distinct product identity shifts. In its first phase (2013–2018), it was a managed Apache Spark service — simplifying distributed data processing for engineers who would otherwise manage their own clusters. In its second phase (2019–2022), it became the Lakehouse platform — Delta Lake + Photon + Unity Catalog transforming cloud object storage into a governed, ACID-compliant, analytics-ready foundation that made the traditional two-system architecture (warehouse + lake) obsolete.

In its current third phase (2023–present), Databricks has become the Data Intelligence Platform — the full-stack enterprise platform that combines analytics, ML, agentic AI (Agent Bricks), operational databases (Lakebase), open-source governance (Unity Catalog), and AI-first data pipelines (Lakeflow) into a single billing relationship and governance model. The IPO narrative explicitly frames this as the disruption of five major categories simultaneously: enterprise analytics (Snowflake), ML platforms (Dataiku, SAS), operational databases (Oracle, PostgreSQL), data integration (Informatica), and AI agent infrastructure (no incumbent).
